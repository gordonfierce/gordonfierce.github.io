Crunching Weight Data

Tue 14 April 2015

I've been keeping track of my weight on someone else's site for a while, but I wanted to grab a csv of the data and take an analytical crack at it.

In [3]:
weight_data = pd.read_csv("../../datapoints.csv", header=None)
weight_data["Weight (lbs)"] = weight_data.pop(1)
weight_data.index = pd.to_datetime(weight_data.pop(0))
weight_data = weight_data.drop(labels=2, axis=1)

The built-in read_csv function in pandas makes short work of importing. I rename the weight column, set the index as the time, and now we have a pandas dataframe.

Out[5]:
<matplotlib.axes._subplots.AxesSubplot at 0x10e276358>

This is a lag plot with a lag of 1 week. The trend upward and to the right shows that there's some autocorrelation in my weight data. This makes sense: my weight in any given week correlates with what I weighed the previous week.

In [6]:
random_data = np.random.randn(360)
rng = pd.date_range('1/1/2013',periods=360,freq='D')
random_frame = pd.DataFrame(random_data, rng)
pd.tools.plotting.lag_plot(random_frame.resample("W"))
Out[6]:
<matplotlib.axes._subplots.AxesSubplot at 0x10e5e48d0>

A lag plot of randomly generated data shows no such trend, instead it clusters arond the middle.

In [7]:
brownian = random_data.cumsum()
brownian_frame = pd.DataFrame(brownian, rng)
pd.tools.plotting.lag_plot(brownian_frame.resample("W"))
Out[7]:
<matplotlib.axes._subplots.AxesSubplot at 0x10e8032b0>

But if we turn the same random data into a random walk, where each data point is the previous datapoint plus a random number, then it becomes autocorrelated, too.
